The Last Lesson

Alphonse Daudet
1. Theme of "The Last Lesson"

The central theme of Alphonse Daudet’s "The Last Lesson" revolves around linguistic chauvinism and the deep psychological impact of cultural imperialism. The story profoundly illustrates how imposing a foreign language on a conquered people strips them of their identity and freedom.

Key Aspects:

  • Language as Identity: The story highlights that one's native language is the essence of their cultural heritage. As M. Hamel rightly points out, when an enslaved people hold fast to their language, it is as if they have the "key to their prison."
  • The Pain of Procrastination: A major sub-theme is the human tendency to postpone learning. The people of Alsace always thought they had "plenty of time" to learn French, only to realize its immense value when it was abruptly snatched away by the Prussians.
  • Loss and Regret: The story beautifully captures the collective sorrow and sudden awakening of the villagers who regret not valuing their mother tongue when they had the chance.
2. Character Sketch: Franz (The Narrator)
  • Initial Carelessness: Franz represents the typical, carefree schoolboy. Initially, he is reluctant to attend school, dreading M. Hamel’s questioning on participles. He prefers spending his time outdoors—seeking birds' eggs or sliding on the Saar—showing his initial ignorance towards education and his cultural heritage.
  • Sudden Transformation: The order from Berlin acts as a catalyst for a sudden, emotional transformation in Franz. The moment he realizes he can no longer learn French, his mindset shifts drastically.
  • Realization of Value: His books, which seemed like a heavy nuisance just moments ago, suddenly feel like "old friends" that he cannot bear to lose. He completely forgets about M. Hamel's strictness and his dreaded iron ruler.
  • Innocence and Maturity: Despite his young age, Franz shows remarkable maturity by the end of the lesson. His silent observation—wondering if the Prussians would force even the pigeons to sing in German—highlights the absolute absurdity of linguistic chauvinism. He represents the voice of innocence realizing the cruel realities of war and occupation.
3. Character Sketch: M. Hamel (The Teacher)
  • A Strict but Dedicated Mentor: M. Hamel is a traditional schoolmaster who has dedicated 40 years of faithful service to the village school in Alsace. Initially perceived by Franz as a "cranky" strict disciplinarian with a terrifying iron ruler, his true character is revealed on his last day.
  • Deep Patriotism: He is a fiercely patriotic man. On the day of the last lesson, he wears his beautiful green coat, frilled shirt, and embroidered black silk cap—clothes usually reserved for inspection and prize days—to show his deep respect for his mother tongue and his country.
  • Honesty and Self-Reflection: Instead of just blaming the students for not learning, M. Hamel shows immense grace by accepting his own faults. He admits that he sometimes gave students holidays when he wanted to go fishing or sent them to water his flowers instead of teaching.
  • Inspiring Figure: In the face of losing his livelihood and being forced to leave his home, he exhibits immense courage, patience, and dignity. He imparts his final lesson with utmost gentleness, calling French the most beautiful, clearest, and most logical language in the world. His final, emotional act of writing "Vive La France!" (Long Live France) on the blackboard cements him as a symbol of immense national pride.
